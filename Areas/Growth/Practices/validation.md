# Validation

Source: "Ricky and Jimmy on Relationships" podcast, validation episode.

Closely related to [[vulnerability]]. Validation is what makes vulnerable
sharing produce closeness instead of conflict. Without it, vulnerability
backfires — even when shared "perfectly."

## What it is

Validation is exploring and acknowledging someone's feelings, thoughts, or
perspective. Respecting their experience. Showing willingness to understand
their point of view through curiosity and listening.

Critical: **validation is not agreement.** You can validate a feeling you
don't share. You can validate without conceding the facts.

> "While I can't agree [with the fact] — that's not my experience — I do
> really care about what you're feeling and what you need right now."

## What it is NOT

- Agreeing with their accusations
- Conceding their facts
- Fixing the problem ("I didn't say that, let me play back the tape")
- Defending yourself first

The instinctive failure mode: partner brings something up, listener hears
*"you are bad / you are wrong as a person,"* and rushes to shut that down
to protect the relationship. **The shutdown is what actually damages the
relationship.** Even if you "win" — they say "okay, you're right, I was
wrong" — they don't feel closer. They feel less safe.

## The two roles — both have skills to learn

Validation needs both ends:

- **The sharer** has to bring it up well — soft startup, vulnerable
  language, name a feeling, no "you always / you never."
- **The receiver** has to meet it with curiosity, attention, and willingness
  to understand before correcting.

Most people overdo one and underdo the other.

## How to bring something up well (sharer)

The Marshall Rosenberg / *Nonviolent Communication* formula:

> When [observation], I felt [feeling]. I'd appreciate [request].

Key moves:

- **Lead with safety.** "Hey, can I talk to you about something on my
  heart? I'm not here to blame you — I love you, this matters. Can you
  just listen for a second?"
- **Use a love-language anchor.** Touch their shoulder, take their hand —
  whatever lowers their guard.
- **Name a feeling, not the behavior.** "I've been feeling sad because I
  don't feel like you've touched me like you used to" >> "You don't touch
  me anymore."
- **Avoid "you always / you never."** They guarantee defensiveness.
- **Pick the timing.** Heavy stuff during connected/calm time, not during
  tension. (Or use a scheduled check-in.)
- **One thing at a time.** No kitchen-sinking — see below.

## How to receive validation well (receiver)

- **Believe them first.** Don't fact-check first. "I'm genuinely curious
  what your experience was."
- **Get curious.** Ask questions. "What happened? What were you feeling?"
- **Body open, attention focused.** Phone away. Body facing them. Eye
  contact (or touch, if eye contact is hard). Nodding. "Mm-hmm."
- **Summarize.** "So when X happened, you felt Y, and you wish Z had
  happened instead. Did I get that right?"
- **Validate before correcting.** Even if their facts are wrong, even if
  you think they're misreading you. Correction can come later — or not at
  all.

## Validating phrases

- "I can see how you felt that way."
- "That makes sense."
- "I never thought about it that way."
- "You're making sense."
- "Your experience matters to me."
- "I want you to feel like we're a team."
- "Let's get on the same page about how to prevent this — I want you to
  feel reassured."
- "It sounds like you felt kind of [dismissed / overlooked / alone]. Would
  you say that's accurate?"
- "Thanks for bringing that up — I know that wasn't easy."
- "While I can't agree with [the fact], I do care about what you're
  feeling."

## Invalidating phrases — the never-say list

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Minimizing | "You're overreacting." "It's not a big deal." "Just get over it." "You're making a big deal about nothing." "It could be worse." "Other people have it worse." "I'm sure it wasn't that bad." |
| Pathologizing | "You're too sensitive." "Stop being so dramatic." "It's all in your head." "You're just being negative." |
| Lecturing | "You shouldn't feel that way." "I wouldn't feel like that if I were you." "That's not a reason to get so upset." "That's just the way life is." |
| Blame-deflecting | "You could have avoided that if you'd just done X." |
| Rejecting the moment | "Why are you trying to ruin a good day?" "You should be grateful." |

If someone says these to *you* and the relationship matters:

- "My feelings aren't up for debate."
- "I recognize you may feel differently — and that's okay, we don't have
  to share the same view."

Don't carry the entire emotional-labor weight of the relationship by
yourself indefinitely. If only one of you is doing this work, the
disconnection compounds.

## Anxious vs. avoidant — important nuance

**Guessing the feeling for them:**

- **Anxious-leaning partner** → loves it. "I bet that made you feel kind of
  abandoned." → "Yes, exactly." Connection.
- **Avoidant-leaning partner** → may push back: "no, I wasn't frustrated."
  Often an autonomy reflex, not real disagreement. **Cover yourself:** "If
  I were you I'd be pretty frustrated. Did you feel that, or something
  else?" Framed as a guess, not a verdict.

**Common avoidant fear:** *"my words will be used against me."* If your
partner is avoidant, you can ask explicitly: "Do you ever feel scared to
talk to me because I might use your words against you?" — and validate
whatever they admit.

**Anxious self-check:** if you tend to use your partner's vulnerable
disclosures back at them in a future fight, that's the thing destroying
their willingness to open up. Vulnerable shares are *to be treasured, not
held as ammo.*

## Defensiveness — name it instead of acting it

When you feel defensive, you can say so out loud instead of acting it out:

> "I'm feeling really defensive right now. What I just heard was X, but I
> don't think that's what you meant. Can you say it differently, or tell me
> what your intention was?"

Naming it prevents auto-pilot escalation. Most conflicts are won or lost
in the microseconds after a complaint lands — pause vs. counterattack.

If you know your partner struggles with defensiveness, you can also ask
*for them*: "Are you feeling defensive right now?" Loving move.

## The kitchen-sink rule

When you're being well-validated, the door cracks open and it's tempting to
unload *everything* you've been holding. Resist. One thing at a time. If
lots is piled up, what's missing is regular check-ins — schedule those
instead of dumping in one moment.

## Weekly check-in

A standing time (~weekly) for:

- Appreciation
- Getting on the same page on logistics
- Anything someone's been holding: *"What's on your heart?"*

Not a designated complaint-fest. Purpose: keep short accounts. Things
don't build up between you.

## Internal vs. external processors

External processor + internal processor = ambush risk. Springing something
heavy on an internal processor feels like you've "come armed with all your
words" and they have nothing.

Healthy move when bringing something up to an internal processor:

> "Here's the gist of what I want to talk about — [one or two sentences].
> Can we sit down on it tomorrow morning so you've had time to think?"

If you're the one being approached and don't have bandwidth right now:
*"I don't want to spend tonight on something heavy. Can we do tomorrow
morning?"* — **and you schedule it.** Pushback without scheduling reads as
avoidance.

## The 15:1 ratio (Gottmans)

Outside of conflict, the ratio of positive interactions (appreciation,
compliments, hugs, kisses, small sweet things) to negative interactions
should be roughly **15:1**. Validation isn't required at that rate — but
if your overall tone toward your partner is mostly complaint, you can't
expect any one complaint to land well.

## Asking your partner what works for *them*

The single most useful question to ask outside of conflict:

> "When we're in a discussion, what helps you feel heard and understood?
> What makes you feel safe enough to be vulnerable with me?"

People will usually tell you. Often they know what they *don't* like ("when
you stare at your phone") more clearly than what they do — that's still
useful.

## Reflection questions

### After a conversation that went badly — as the receiver

1. Did I lead with curiosity, or with correction?
2. Did I summarize and verify before responding?
3. Did I assume the worst about the meaning of what they said? Did I take
   *"you did X"* as *"you are bad"*?
4. Was my body language receptive (phone down, body facing, eye contact
   or touch)?
5. Did I validate *before* — or *instead of* — agreeing?
6. If I felt defensive, did I name it, or act on it?

### After a conversation that went badly — as the sharer

1. Did I bring this up at a good time?
2. Did I lead with safety ("I love you, not blaming you")?
3. Did I name a feeling, or did I lead with "you always / you never"?
4. Did I bring up *one* thing, or did I kitchen-sink?
5. Did I want them to *understand me*, or to *agree with me*? (If the
   latter, it wasn't really a vulnerable share — see [[vulnerability]] on
   pitching vs. sharing pain.)

## Long-term self-check

- Where am I currently the receiver who jumps to defense before curiosity?
- Where am I currently the sharer who brings things up hot?
- Is our positive-to-negative ratio anywhere near 15:1?
- Have I asked my partner what specific phrases land as validating for
  them?
- Are vulnerable disclosures being used as ammo in later fights — by me,
  or by them?

## References

- *Nonviolent Communication* — Marshall Rosenberg. The "When X, I felt Y,
  I'd appreciate Z" formula and the general approach.
- John & Julie Gottman — research on the 15:1 ratio and patterns that
  predict relationship trajectory.
